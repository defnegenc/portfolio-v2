# Statement of Purpose — Ph.D. in Computer Science at Stanford University

**Defne Genç**

AI has transformed what computing can do: understanding context, adapting to individuals, and learning from patterns. Yet our interfaces remain locked in old paradigms such as rigid UIs and optimization-centric metrics. Our interfaces for health, learning, and productivity constantly highlight gaps and failures, which has negative impacts on long-term behavior change, mental health, and self-perception. I see two critical problems with current systems: rigid interfaces that fail to serve diverse populations, and negative feedback loops that undermine the very outcomes they're designed to support. I aim to build adaptive ubiquitous computing systems that learn from ambient patterns, adapt to individual differences, and surface insights about positive behaviors people are unaware of, revealing hidden strengths. Drawing on ubiquitous computing principles that technologies should be ambient, unintrusive, and anticipate our needs, I aim to establish design principles for adaptive interfaces that enhance human capability while maintaining safety and human agency, exploring how technology can shift self-perception in ways that build confidence and support long-term behavior change.

My path to HCI began with wet lab research at the Kuo Lab in Stanford Medicine studying ovarian cancer organoids, where rigorous experimental protocols taught me systematic investigation. Yet I found myself drawn to research questions centered on human behavior, leading me to pursue Symbolic Systems and Human-Computer Interaction. At Stanford with Prof. Landay, I co-designed and evaluated Bloom, an LLM-based physical activity coaching intervention. My contributions spanned early-stage design through full-stack implementation: I led red-teaming for our agent, created a taxonomy of harms validated on a 600-example benchmark achieving >96% recall, designed our ambient display, and completed qualitative coding of all offboarding interviews. I was heavily involved in recruitment and had the most influence on UI design, safety filters, and frontend implementation, making key decisions on app architecture and user experience. The result: treatment participants showed larger mindset shifts (+1.2 versus +0.8 points in beliefs about activity benefits), yet no single conversational strategy drove these shifts consistently. The system's flexibility allowed different participants to benefit from different aspects: activity reframing, goal alignment, or acknowledgment of existing efforts. Even those with shallow engagement showed meaningful changes, suggesting that adaptive, personalized representations could shift self-perception without requiring deep conversational interaction. This experience crystallized my research direction.

**Rigid interfaces.** Current systems fail populations with diverse needs not from user deficiency but from design assumptions that privilege certain cognitive styles and contexts. A student struggling with dense textbooks may simply process information differently, or someone with dyslexia and ADHD finds habit trackers designed for neurotypical attention spans become obstacles rather than aids. This rigidity isn't just a problem for marginalized populations — it's a universal design challenge. Even "ideal" users find themselves constrained by interfaces that assume singular contexts and interaction modes. AI combined with ubiquitous computing principles suggests we're no longer bound to this model. Systems can now understand semantic goals and adapt to richer context, processing natural language intentions and adjusting to how you're engaging with information in real time.

**Negative feedback loops.** Beyond flexibility, how we surface information shapes how we see ourselves. Take sleep tracking as an example of what the literature calls "orthosomnia." Despite being health-conscious, I found that tracking my sleep produced harm rather than benefit. Consistently surfacing "poor" sleep resulted in anxiety that prevented sleep itself, creating a paradox where measurement interfered with the outcome I was trying to improve. This points to a design opportunity: can we design systems that amplify what's working rather than fixating on what isn't? Bloom demonstrated this potential. By highlighting aspects of participants' routines (like gardening) that counted as exercise, we enabled participants to realize they were doing more than they had given themselves credit for. Although we observed no statistically significant increases in short-term physical activity, the mindset shifts point to potential for longer-term change.

The path forward involves interfaces that adapt both what they surface and how they surface it. For instance, if a student struggles with 90% of new material but demonstrates a strong grasp of one concept, that strength could be surfaced as an ambient display for persistent visual encouragement, or as a contextual scaffold that builds bridges to harder topics. When technology helps us see our own competence and progress in forms matched to how we process information, it can create upward spirals of confidence and capability. I intend to study how AI-enabled ubiquitous computing systems can address both problems: by discovering the right forms and representations to surface positive patterns we are unaware of. The challenge is finding representations that resonate with different cognitive styles and interaction preferences. I hope to explore these questions through functional prototypes integrating multiple sensing modalities (wearables, spatial sensors, interaction logs, tangible artifacts) in passive longitudinal studies. Beyond chat interfaces, I'm interested in adaptive primitives: contextual interventions that emerge based on user state, editable ambient widgets, spatial design, and tangible objects that carry personal meaning in multi-week deployments to measure shifts in self-efficacy, perceived control, self-awareness, confidence, and anxiety.

At Stanford, I share Prof. Landay's focus on centering the human experience in developing AI systems while accounting for unintended externalities. His work on context-aware computing and ubicomp interfaces provides a foundation for exploring how spatial awareness, ambient sensing, and multimodal output can create adaptive systems. I am also drawn to Prof. Bernstein's work investigating agential systems and storing human experiences in natural language, a method I intend to use in my research. Together, their work points to a future where agents not only sense and adapt to context, but also reason about a person's lived experience over time. This is the direction I intend to push in my PhD.
